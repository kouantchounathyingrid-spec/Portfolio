What I do in the laboratory

Analytical capability from sample to interpreted result — non-destructive, multi-element, and validated against reference methods.

X-ray fluorescence (XRF)

Non-destructive multi-element analysis on the Shimadzu EDX-900. Calibration across characteristic emission lines, sensitivity and detection-limit determination, and quantification down to 3×10⁻⁷ mol/L.

Classical analytical methods

Titrimetry, gravimetry and wet chemistry — the reference techniques used to confirm that an instrumental result is correct, not merely precise.

Method development & calibration

Building calibration curves with linear response (R² ≥ 0.98), applying the standard-addition method to real matrices, and optimising measurement workflows for reproducibility.

Matrix effects & interference modelling

Mathematical models that account for intrinsic fluorescence and inter-element absorption, with coefficients estimated from full factorial 2ᵏ experiments and validated statistically.

Quality assurance & GLP

Good Laboratory Practice, laboratory documentation, and safety compliance — maintained across every stage of the analytical workflow.

Sample preparation & standards

Preparing samples, model solutions and standards across concentration ranges from 1×10⁻⁴ to 1×10⁻¹ mol/L, for reliable quantitative interpretation.

/ Selected research

Publications & findings

My research centres on XRF as a non-destructive technique for multi-element analysis — developing methods, quantifying detection limits, and resolving the matrix effects that stand between a spectrum and a trustworthy number.

01

Simultaneous X-ray fluorescence determination of iron, copper, nickel, chromium and zinc cations in aqueous solutions

Bulletin of SUSU, Series “Chemistry” 2026

A method for the simultaneous determination of five metal cations, built on a mathematical model relating fluorescence intensity to sample composition — one equation per characteristic wavelength, accounting for the blank experiment, each metal's fluorescence, and absorption by accompanying components. Model coefficients were estimated from a full factorial 2ᵏ experiment and checked for significance and adequacy. Correctness was confirmed on mixed model solutions and by comparing galvanic-effluent results against alternative methods.

5 metals, simultaneous Full factorial 2ᵏ design Validated vs. alternative methods

02

X-ray fluorescence determination of Br, I, Mo, W and Cr in aqueous solutions

Bashkir Chemical Journal 2026 Accepted · No. 2

Calibration curves were constructed for each element across the emission lines available on the Shimadzu EDX-900, giving linear response (R² ≥ 0.98) over 1×10⁻⁴–1×10⁻¹ mol/L. Detection limits ranged from 3×10⁻⁷ mol/L for molybdenum (Kα) to 4×10⁻⁵ mol/L for iodine (Kα). Strong asymmetric interference emerged — iodine suppressed bromine more than the reverse, tungsten interfered with molybdenum more than the reverse — intensifying at higher concentrations, so real samples were analysed by standard addition. The method quantified molybdenum in the multivitamin Duovit, and bromine and iodine in sea salt, consistent with reference values.

R² ≥ 0.98 LOD 3×10⁻⁷ mol/L (Mo, Kα) Random error 1–3%

03

X-ray fluorescence determination of Cr, Ni and Cu in galvanic wastewater

eLIBRARY ID 91584366 · EDN IKQEXB

Galvanic manufacturing generates 600–800 m³/day of wastewater carrying toxic heavy-metal ions — Cr(VI), Ni(II), Cu(II) — with significant mutagenic and carcinogenic risk, and insufficient treatment leads to long-term public-health impact. This work proposes an XRF approach for determining all three simultaneously: model solutions at 0.2–10 mg/L, calibration on the EDX-900, evaluation of mutual spectral interference, and a mathematical model covering both intrinsic fluorescence and matrix effects. Tested on model mixtures and untreated galvanic wastewater, it delivers analysis fast enough for real-time effluent monitoring.

0.2–10 mg/L range Real-time effluent monitoring

04

Behaviour of lead uranyl oxyhydroxides in saturated aqueous solutions across the pH range

eLIBRARY ID 68491676 · EDN HNHNAY

A study of Pb[(UO₂)O₂(OH)₂]·H₂O and Pb₂[(UO₂)₅O₆(OH)₂]·2H₂O, showing that solution acidity strongly governs the composition and structure of equilibrium solid phases and the speciation of U(VI) and Pb(II). Both compounds remain stable between pH 2 and 13.2; outside that window they break down into amorphous products below pH 2 and sodium diuranate under strong alkali. Solubility spans orders of magnitude — a minimum of 10⁻¹¹ mol/L in mildly alkaline solution, rising to 10⁻² mol/L in strong acid. The data supported a quantitative physicochemical model with equilibrium constants, Gibbs formation functions, theoretical solubility curves and phase diagrams.

Stable pH 2–13.2 Solubility 10⁻¹¹–10⁻² mol/L Phase diagrams derived

05

Applicability of XRF for determining Br, I, Mo, W and Cr in aqueous solutions

eLIBRARY ID 50816895 · EDN EQUYZL

An evaluation of XRF's applicability to five elements in aqueous media. Sensitivity and detection limits were established with standard solutions — molybdenum lowest at 3×10⁻⁷ mol/L, iodine highest at 4×10⁻⁵ mol/L. Interference studies revealed stronger mutual effects between bromine and iodine, and between molybdenum and tungsten, particularly at low analyte concentrations. Accounting for these matrix effects, real samples were analysed by standard addition: molybdenum in Duovit, and bromine and iodine in sea salt, both consistent with reference data.

5 elements evaluated Standard-addition method
