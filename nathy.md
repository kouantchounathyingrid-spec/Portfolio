https://doi.org/10.14529/chem260207; Here, a method for simultaneous X-ray fluorescence determination of iron, copper, nickel, chromium, and zinc cations in aqueous solutions is proposed. The method uses a mathematical model describing the dependence of the X-ray fluorescence intensity on the sample composition. The model included five equations with unknown concentrations of each metal – one for each characteristic wavelength. The intensity of the blank experiment, the fluorescence intensities of each metal, and the absorption of radiation by accompanying components were taken into account in the equations. The model coefficients were estimated based on the results of a type 2ᵏ full factorial experiment. The significance of the coefficient estimates and the adequacy of the model were checked. The relationship between the sensitivity coefficients in the regression equations and the molar masses of the determined metals was shown. A significant interfering influence of individual elements on the determination results of each other was established. The correctness of the analysis using the proposed method was confirmed by studying mixed model solutions and comparing the results of the galvanic effluents analysis with data from alternative methods.

Bashkir Chemical Journal. 2026 (accepted for publication in No. 2) ; This study develops and evaluates an Xray fluorescence (XRF) method for determining bromine, iodine, molybdenum, tungsten, and chromium in aqueous solutions. Calibration curves were constructed for each element using different characteristic emission lines available on the Shimadzu EDX900 spectrometer. The method demonstrated linear analytical responses (R² ≥ 0.98) across the concentration range 1×10⁻⁴–1×10⁻¹ mol/L. Detection limits varied significantly among elements, with the lowest achieved for molybdenum (3×10⁻⁷ mol/L, Kα line) and the highest for iodine (4×10⁻⁵ mol/L, Kα line). Relative random errors ranged from 1–3% for most elements, increasing to 6% for iodine due to its lower signal intensity.

Strong mutual interference effects were observed: iodine suppressed bromine signals more strongly than the reverse, and tungsten interfered with molybdenum determination more significantly than molybdenum interfered with tungsten. These matrix effects intensified at higher analyte concentrations. Because of this, realsample analysis was performed using the standard addition method. The approach successfully quantified molybdenum in the multivitamin supplement Duovit and bromine and iodine in sea salt, with results consistent with reference values.

Overall, the study confirms that XRF can be effectively applied to aqueous solutions despite matrixrelated challenges, and that the Kα line of molybdenum provides the highest sensitivity among the examined elements.

eLIBRARY ID: 91584366; EDN: IKQEXB: Galvanic manufacturing generates large volumes of wastewater (up to 600–800 m³/day) containing toxic heavy‑metal ions such as Cr(VI), Ni(II), and Cu(II), which pose significant mutagenic and carcinogenic risks. Insufficient treatment efficiency leads to environmental contamination and long‑term public‑health impacts, highlighting the need for rapid and selective multicomponent analytical methods. This work proposes an X‑ray fluorescence (XRF) approach for the simultaneous determination of Cr, Ni, and Cu in aqueous solutions. Model solutions (0.2–10 mg/L) were prepared, calibration curves were constructed using a Shimadzu EDX‑900 spectrometer, and mutual spectral interferences were evaluated. A mathematical model accounting for both intrinsic fluorescence and matrix effects was developed, with coefficients obtained from a full factorial 2ᵏ experiment and validated statistically. The model enables calculation of metal concentrations from XRF measurements at three characteristic wavelengths. The method was tested on model mixtures and untreated galvanic wastewater, demonstrating accurate determination of all three metals. The proposed approach provides fast multicomponent analysis suitable for real‑time monitoring of industrial effluents. 

 eLIBRARY ID: 68491676; EDN: HNHNAY: This study examines the behavior of two lead uranyl oxyhydroxide compounds, Pb[(UO₂)O₂(OH)₂]·H₂O and Pb₂[(UO₂)₅O₆(OH)₂]·2H₂O, in saturated aqueous solutions across a wide pH range. The results show that solution acidity strongly influences the composition and structure of the equilibrium solid phases, as well as the concentrations and speciation of U(VI) and Pb(II). Both compounds exhibit broad stability intervals between pH 2 and 13.2. Outside this range, their structures break down, forming new, more stable phases: amorphous products in strongly acidic media (pH < 2) and sodium diuranate (Na₂U₂O₇) under strongly alkaline conditions due to hydrolysis. Solubility varies by several orders of magnitude depending on pH, reaching a minimum of 10⁻¹¹ mol/L in mildly alkaline solutions and increasing to 10⁻⁶ mol/L in strong alkali and 10⁻² mol/L in strong acid. Experimental data were used to construct a quantitative physicochemical model describing heterogeneous dissolution equilibria alongside homogeneous speciation of U(VI) and Pb(II). Equilibrium constants, Gibbs formation functions, theoretical solubility curves, and phase diagrams for U(VI) and Pb(II) were calculated for the studied systems. 

 eLIBRARY ID: 50816895; EDN: EQUYZL: This work evaluated the applicability of X‑ray fluorescence analysis (XRF) for determining bromine, iodine, molybdenum, tungsten, and chromium in aqueous solutions. Sensitivity and detection limits were established using standard solutions, with molybdenum showing the lowest detection limit (3×10⁻⁷ mol/L) and iodine the highest (4×10⁻⁵ mol/L). Interference studies revealed stronger mutual effects between bromine and iodine, and between molybdenum and tungsten, especially at low analyte concentrations. The determined molybdenum content in Duovit and bromine and iodine levels in sea salt were consistent with reference data.
